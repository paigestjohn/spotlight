# Budget & Revenue Monitoring

How to investigate government budgets and extractive industry revenues -- the investigative questions to ask, where to find budget data, and how to spot discrepancies between what governments promise and what actually reaches communities.

## When to Use This

You're investigating how a government collects and spends public money, particularly revenues from oil, gas, or mining. Or you're trying to determine whether public funds are reaching their intended destination -- schools, clinics, infrastructure.

## Understanding the Resource Curse

Countries rich in natural resources often suffer worse economic and political outcomes than resource-poor countries -- a phenomenon known as the "resource curse." Understanding this context is essential for investigating extractive industry finances.

### Why Resource Wealth Causes Problems

1. **Underemployment:** Oil and gas are capital-intensive, not labor-intensive. They require investment but don't create many jobs. In Azerbaijan, the oil sector accounts for a significant portion of GDP but less than 1% of employment.

2. **Price shocks:** Resource-rich countries become dependent on volatile commodity prices. When prices crash, government revenues collapse, causing political instability and cuts to public services.

3. **Dutch Disease:** Large inflows of foreign currency from resource sales drive up the local currency, making other exports (agriculture, manufacturing) uncompetitive. The economy becomes dependent on the single resource.

4. **Non-renewability:** Extracted resources eventually run out. Countries often spend revenues on ongoing programs instead of strategic investments for the future. In Azerbaijan, reserves were expected to be exhausted by 2024. In Kazakhstan, by mid-century.

5. **Poor governance shield:** Abundant resource revenues allow leaders to avoid accountability. They can fund patronage networks and suppress opposition without taxing citizens -- removing the accountability link between taxation and representation.

### The Investigative Significance

The resource curse creates a pattern: wealth concentrates at the top, governments resist transparency, extractive revenues are kept off-budget or in opaque oil funds, and communities see little benefit. This pattern produces investigative stories.

## Key Investigative Questions

### Revenue Side: How Much Is the Government Earning?

- What are the production-sharing agreements between the government and extractive companies? Are they public?
- What are the production volumes, prices charged, and revenues earned?
- How much is transferred from oil/gas funds into the national budget?
- Is the tax system fair? Are extractive industries paying their share, or is the tax burden falling disproportionately on the poor?
- Are revenues being kept off-budget in separate oil funds that avoid legislative scrutiny?

### Expenditure Side: Where Is the Money Going?

- Does the budgeted money actually arrive at local schools and clinics?
- What is it buying? What effect is it having on people's lives?
- Are extractive revenues directed toward long-term national investment, or consumed by ongoing programs and government apparatus?
- Are oil fund expenditures matching their stated purpose (strategic investment vs. deficit financing)?
- Has the government raided special funds for political purposes (arms purchases, election-year spending)?

### Fairness: Is It a Fair Share?

- How do the government's terms compare to what other countries receive?
- What provisions exist to capture windfall profits when prices rise?
- Who owns the extraction infrastructure -- and what happens to it when resources run out?

## Obtaining Budget Data

Budget data is hard to get. Many countries have no tradition of publishing it. Use these strategies:

### Official Sources
- **Government budget documents:** Often published online by finance ministries, but may be incomplete, late, or in unintelligible formats.
- **Legislative budget submissions:** The executive's budget proposal to parliament. Getting this before approval is critical -- "If you get the information when the budget is nearly approved, it is too late."
- **Audit reports:** National audit offices produce annual reviews of government spending. These often reveal discrepancies.
- **EITI reports:** Countries participating in the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative publish annual reports on revenues received from extractive companies. Available at eiti.org.

### FOI Requests
Freedom of information laws exist in 100+ countries. Use them to request:
- Financial company reports filed with government
- Government budget spending breakdowns
- Revenue data from specific departments or funds
- Production-sharing agreements

### Community-Level Research
When national data is opaque, go to the community level:
- Track whether budgeted funds reach schools, clinics, and infrastructure projects
- Compare what the budget allocates to a district with what actually arrives there
- Interview community members, teachers, health workers about what resources they receive
- This is the Uganda Debt Network model -- grassroots monitors measuring mattress deliveries, teacher attendance, road quality

### Unofficial Sources
- **Leaks from officials:** Government employees who want reform or are dissatisfied may provide documents. Protect sources carefully.
- **International financial institutions:** World Bank, IMF, and regional development banks often have better data than governments themselves. IMF fiscal transparency reviews are published online.
- **Corporate filings:** In countries where extractive companies are publicly traded elsewhere, their SEC or Companies House filings may reveal revenue data their host governments won't publish.
- **Investigative partnerships:** OCCRP, ICIJ, and GIJN can connect you with reporters in other countries who may have access to relevant data.

## Analyzing Budget Discrepancies

The most impactful investigations track the gap between what's budgeted and what's delivered:

1. **Get the budget allocation** for a specific program, district, or fund
2. **Track the money downstream** -- from national budget to ministry to district to community
3. **Measure what arrives** -- through official reports, FOI requests, or community monitoring
4. **Calculate the gap** -- the difference between allocation and delivery is your story
5. **Identify where money disappears** -- at which level does the gap appear? This points to where the problem (corruption, mismanagement, diversion) occurs

### Budget Analysis Techniques

- **Trend analysis:** Track spending categories over time. A shrinking education budget while defense spending grows tells a story.
- **Per-capita analysis:** Convert budget figures to per-person amounts to make them meaningful to the public.
- **Revenue dependency analysis:** What percentage of the budget comes from extractive revenues? High dependency = high vulnerability to price shocks.
- **Cross-country comparison:** How does this country's revenue share compare to similar resource-producing countries?
- **Projection analysis:** Challenge government assumptions. If they project oil revenues for 50 years but reserves will last 20, the fiscal plan is built on fiction.

## Revenue Transparency Frameworks

These frameworks create investigative leverage -- they represent commitments governments have made that you can hold them to:

### EITI (Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative)
- Countries voluntarily join and commit to publishing extractive revenue data
- EITI reports reveal what companies pay and what governments receive
- Discrepancies between the two are stories
- Implementation reports assess whether countries are meeting their commitments
- Use as a benchmark: if a country joined EITI but isn't publishing data, that's a story

### Publish What You Pay (PWYP)
- International coalition calling for mandatory disclosure of payments by extractive companies to governments
- Tracks which companies and countries are transparent and which aren't
- Their research and reports can provide leads and context

### IDI Follow The Money Toolkit
- INTOSAI Development Initiative toolkit for investigating development finance
- Covers DFI-funded projects (World Bank, IFC, Asian Development Bank)
- Useful for investigating whether international development funds reach their intended recipients
- Project disclosure portals: World Bank, IFC, ADB all maintain searchable databases of funded projects

## WARNING: Safety Considerations

Investigating extractive industry finances can be dangerous. Governments that depend on resource revenues to maintain power resist transparency. In some countries, civil society monitors have faced arbitrary arrests, extra-judicial trials, torture, and worse for asking questions about oil revenue. Assess the political context before beginning and take appropriate security precautions. For OPSEC guidance, see `/osint` (opsec-basics).
